Stanford University NEMS
Kim Harrison

Research Focus:
Nanoelectromechanical (NEM) Relay System Integration
The design of Nanoelectromechanical (NEM) Relays has become a popular topic of investigation because of their low subthreshold slope and zero leakage current in the off-state compared to established CMOS transistors. Furthermore, advanced materials, design and process techniques allow NEM Relays to be fabricated and operated in conjunction with other devices. The integration of NEM Relays can mitigate unnecessary power loss, reduce device footprint and increase the versatility of device design. My research involves the design, fabrication, and characterization of NEM relays for logic, routing and power delivery. My work focuses on two applications: 1) low-power/small-footprint FPGA routing using CMOS-compatible NEM Relays, and 2) optimization of device reliability and behavior through mechanical design.
Field Programmable Gate Arrays (FPGAs) allow more versatility and lower design costs than ASICs. However, FPGAs suffer from higher energy losses and larger footprints due to on-chip programmable routing arrays. It has been shown that the performance of the routing array can be improved by replacing each NMOS transistor/SRAM cell with a single relay. However, NEM relay based routing arrays suffer from poor relay reliability and high contact resistance. In order to improve the yield, consistency and reliability of batches of NEM relays, I am investigating new contact materials (such as Ruthenium and Titanium Nitride), redundant mechanical design to improve statistical behavior, and wafer-scale packaging techniques. I am experimenting with low-temperature relay fabrication processes using amorphous silicon and silicon germanium in order to prevent destruction of CMOS devices in back end of line processing, in collaboration with Berkeley's Nanolab.
My work requires an understanding of nano-scale Ohmic contact behavior. As the relays scale to smaller sizes, the true contact area increasingly depends on fragile asperity-asperity interactions, which are subject to extremely high pressures and temperatures. These extreme conditions promote mechanical deformation and chemical reactions at the contact, thus leading to inconsistent behavior, fast degradation and unexpected failure. I am involved in designing novel mechanical structures which avoid some of the negative effects of scaling, but without better understanding of contact phenomena, these efforts can provide only limited improvements. Therefore, I am exploring new methods of analyzing the temperatures, pressures and chemical composition at the contact surface in situ. These methods could allow designers to avoid failure by welding or oxidation, and I have proposed devices which even depend on welding, such as latches.
